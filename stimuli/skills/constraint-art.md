# Constraint Art Reference

## The Generative Power of Constraints

- **Why constraints work:** An empty page has infinite possibilities, which is paralyzing. A page where you can't use the letter 'e' has fewer possibilities, but your mind — forced off its habitual paths — finds combinations it never would have otherwise. Constraints don't limit creativity; they redirect it.
- **Constraint satisfaction vs. constraint exploitation:** In computer science, constraint satisfaction finds any valid solution. In art, the goal is to make the constraint invisible (elegant satisfaction) or to make the constraint visible and meaningful (exploitation). The highest form: the constraint and the content are the same thing.
- **Productive vs. arbitrary constraints:** A productive constraint shapes the work in ways that serve its meaning. Writing about loss in a villanelle (obsessive repetition) is productive. Writing about loss using only words from a furniture catalog is arbitrary — unless the loss is about a home. The best constraint art makes the constraint feel inevitable.

## Oulipo Techniques

- **Lipogram:** Writing that avoids one or more letters. Georges Perec's "La Disparition" is a 300-page novel without the letter 'e' — in French, where 'e' is the most common letter. The absence itself becomes the subject (disparition = disappearance). The constraint mirrors the content: something essential is missing.
- **N+7 (also S+7):** Replace every noun in a text with the noun appearing 7 entries later in a dictionary. "The cat sat on the mat" → "The catcall sat on the matter." Produces uncanny, surreal text that retains the grammar and rhythm of the original while displacing its meaning.
- **Prisoner's constraint:** Also called the BOX constraint. Only use letters without ascenders or descenders (a, c, e, m, n, o, r, s, u, v, w, x, z). Every letter fits within the x-height. Named for prisoners communicating through grilles. The visual uniformity of the text IS part of the work.
- **Univocalics:** A text using only one vowel. Christian Bök's "Eunoia" has five chapters, each using exclusively one vowel. Chapter A: "Awkward grammar appalls a craftsman." The phonetic character of each vowel shapes the mood of its chapter.
- **Clinamen:** The deliberate violation of a constraint at a calculated point. Not a failure but a strategic deviation. In a lipogram, the forbidden letter appears once — and that appearance carries enormous weight. The rule makes the exception meaningful.

## Algorithmic and Procedural Constraints

- **Sol LeWitt's wall drawings:** "Draw 10,000 lines at random within a 1m square." The instruction IS the artwork. Different people executing the same instruction produce different results. The artist authors the system, not the output. Every generative algorithm is a Sol LeWitt instruction.
- **Brian Eno's Oblique Strategies:** Cards with gnomic prompts ("Use an old idea," "Honor thy error as a hidden intention," "What would your closest friend do?"). Not instructions but reframings. Pull a card when stuck. The randomness breaks the mental loop.
- **Fluxus event scores:** "Composition 1960 #10: Draw a straight line and follow it." (La Monte Young). "Hide and Seek Piece: Hide. When everyone has gone, come out." Minimalist instructions that blur art, performance, and daily life. The score is complete; the performance is open.
- **One-rule systems:** Langton's Ant — one rule, emergent highway patterns. Rule 110 cellular automaton — Turing-complete from one rule. Life — four rules, infinite complexity. The art is in choosing a rule that produces complexity without chaos.
- **Wave Function Collapse:** An algorithm that generates patterns satisfying local adjacency constraints. Given a set of tiles and rules about which tiles can neighbor which, it fills a grid. Used for procedural level generation but also produces stunning visual patterns. The constraints are the aesthetic — different constraint sets produce different visual vocabularies.

## The Mathematical Sublime

- **Fractals as constraint art:** A fractal is a simple rule applied recursively. The Mandelbrot set: z → z² + c, iterated. The infinite complexity emerges from a constraint you can write on a napkin. The gap between the simplicity of the rule and the complexity of the result IS the aesthetic experience.
- **Fibonacci in composition:** Not just the golden ratio — Fibonacci numbers as structural constraint. A poem with stanzas of 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8 lines. A piece of music where phrase lengths follow the sequence. The sequence feels "right" without the audience knowing why.
- **Symmetry and symmetry-breaking:** Group theory classifies all possible symmetries. The 17 wallpaper groups exhaust the possibilities for 2D repeating patterns (every tiled bathroom floor is one of exactly 17 types). Art lives in the tension between symmetry and its violation.
- **Gödel numbering as art:** Assign numbers to symbols, encode statements as integers. A poem that is also a valid number in some encoding. A melody whose MIDI values spell a message in ASCII. The work exists simultaneously in two interpretive frames.

## Constraint Prompts

Fifteen specific constraints, each applicable to any creative domain:

1. **The subtraction constraint:** Remove the most important element from your medium. Music without melody. Prose without verbs. A game without a win condition. What remains?
2. **The translation constraint:** Take a work from one domain and translate it to another, preserving structure but not content. A sonnet's rhyme scheme becomes a color pattern. A fugue's structure becomes a dialogue between characters.
3. **The time constraint:** Create a work that must be experienced in exactly 60 seconds. Not "about" 60 seconds — exactly. What do you prioritize? What do you cut?
4. **The material constraint:** Use only what's within arm's reach right now. The constraint is your specific, contingent physical reality. A poem from the words visible on your desk. Music from the sounds in your room.
5. **The iteration constraint:** Start with one element. Each iteration, you may add one element and must modify one existing element. After 10 iterations, what have you built?
6. **The audience constraint:** Create for an audience of one specific, named entity that is not human — a houseplant, a particular satellite, the Pacific Ocean. How does the audience reshape the work?
7. **The failure constraint:** Design something that must fail in a beautiful, interesting, or meaningful way. The failure IS the work.
8. **The memory constraint:** Create a work that can only be experienced once and must be remembered — it cannot be recorded, saved, or revisited. How does impermanence change what you make?
9. **The parasitic constraint:** Your work must attach to an existing work, depending on it for context. A footnote to a famous text. A soundtrack for a painting. An epilogue to someone else's story.
10. **The exhaustion constraint:** Enumerate all possibilities. Every two-word sentence using one-syllable words. Every melody using four notes and eight beats. The exhaustive catalog reveals the shape of the possibility space.
11. **The entropy constraint:** Begin with order; each step increases disorder. A perfectly structured poem that accumulates typographical errors. A melody that gradually decomposes into noise. Entropy as narrative arc.
12. **The collaboration constraint:** Create a work where odd-numbered elements follow one rule and even-numbered elements follow a contradictory rule. The tension between the two systems IS the composition.
13. **The found constraint:** Open the nearest book to page 47. The first complete sentence is your constraint. Apply it literally.
14. **The self-referential constraint:** The work must describe its own construction. A poem about writing a poem under this constraint. Code that outputs its own source with commentary.
15. **The impossibility constraint:** Define a constraint that cannot be perfectly satisfied. Pursue it anyway. The gap between the constraint and the closest achievable result is where the art lives.

## Historical Lineage

- **Dada (1916):** Chance operations, cut-up technique, anti-art. Tristan Tzara: cut words from a newspaper, shake them in a bag, pull them out. The result is a poem. The refusal of intentionality as an artistic stance.
- **Surrealist games (1920s):** Exquisite Corpse — collaborative drawing/writing where each participant can't see the previous contribution. Automatic writing — write without conscious control. The unconscious as creative engine.
- **Oulipo (1960):** Ouvroir de littérature potentielle — Workshop of Potential Literature. Raymond Queneau, Georges Perec, Italo Calvino. Mathematics as literary method. Constraints as generative tools, not restrictions. "Rats who build the labyrinth from which they plan to escape."
- **Conceptual art (1960s-70s):** The idea is the work; execution is secondary. Sol LeWitt: "The idea becomes a machine that makes the art." This is the philosophical foundation of generative art.
- **Generative art (1960s-present):** Vera Molnár's computer drawings. Manfred Mohr's hypercube projections. Casey Reas and Ben Fry's Processing. The constraint is an algorithm; the output is visual. The artist authors the system.
