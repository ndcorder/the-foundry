# Documentary Forms Reference

## Creative Non-Fiction Techniques

- **The scenic method:** Reconstruct real events as vivid scenes with dialogue, sensory detail, and pacing — the techniques of fiction applied to fact. The writer was there, or interviewed someone who was. John McPhee can make the geology of roadcuts read like a thriller. The trick: selection. Reality has infinite detail; the writer chooses which details to render.
- **The braided essay:** Two or more seemingly unrelated threads woven together until the reader discovers the connection. Thread A might be a personal memory; Thread B might be the history of an industry; Thread C might be a scientific concept. The braiding IS the argument — the juxtaposition creates meaning that direct statement can't.
- **Immersion journalism:** The writer enters a world and reports from inside it. Barbara Ehrenreich waitressing for minimum wage ("Nickel and Dimed"). George Plimpton quarterbacking for the Lions. Ted Conover riding freight trains. The writer's body and experience become the instrument of research.
- **Gonzo journalism (Hunter S. Thompson):** The reporter is not just present but central — their subjective experience, distortions, and biases are the story. The boundary between observer and participant dissolves. The unreliability is the point: all reporting is subjective; gonzo makes the subjectivity visible.

## The Essay as Experiment

- **Montaigne's original project:** "Essai" = attempt, trial. Not a thesis defended but a mind exploring. The essay follows the writer's thinking, including its wrong turns and reversals. The form models how thought actually works — associative, recursive, self-correcting.
- **The lyric essay:** Favors image, association, and fragmentation over argument and narrative. More concerned with pattern than plot. White space between sections. The reader assembles meaning from juxtaposition. Jenny Offill's "Dept. of Speculation" lives here — novel-length but assembled from fragments.
- **The hermit crab essay:** Non-fiction that borrows the form of another genre. An essay structured as a recipe. A memoir shaped like a field guide. A personal history formatted as a table of contents. The borrowed form constrains and reveals — why THIS form for THIS content?
- **The segmented essay:** Numbered sections or titled fragments. No transitions. The white space between segments does the connective work. The reader must leap the gaps. Each segment is self-contained; the sequence creates emergent meaning.
- **The essay as list:** "Thirteen Ways of Looking at a Blackbird" (Stevens, though that's a poem). "36 Tattoos" (Elissa Washuta). The list form implies that enumeration IS understanding — or that it isn't, and the incompleteness is the point.

## Found Documents and Archival Art

- **Found document fiction:** Fiction presented as non-fiction documents — letters, reports, transcripts, medical records, encyclopedia entries. The form authenticates the content. The reader occupies the position of someone who found these documents. The gaps between documents are where the reader's imagination works.
- **Archival art (making fiction from real archives):** Using real historical documents as raw material. The gaps, silences, and absences in the archive become the subject. What was deemed not worth recording? Whose voices are missing? The archivist's categories and biases become visible.
- **The unreliable narrator in non-fiction:** All narrators are unreliable — memory is reconstruction, not recording. The honest non-fiction writer acknowledges this. Mary Karr in "The Liars' Club" tells you when her memory is uncertain. The admission of unreliability paradoxically increases trust.
- **Oral history as literature:** Svetlana Alexievich's "Voices from Chernobyl" — testimony arranged, edited, and sequenced into a choral narrative. The individual voice contains the collective experience. The editor's work (selection, sequencing, juxtaposition) is invisible but constitutive.
- **Sebald's method:** W.G. Sebald embedded photographs in his prose — blurry, uncaptioned, sometimes unrelated to the text. The images create an evidential texture, a feeling that this really happened, while simultaneously undermining that certainty. The reader can't tell what's documented and what's invented. The ambiguity is the point.

## Data as Narrative

- **Data journalism as storytelling:** Not just charts and statistics but the story the data tells. The best data journalism has characters (specific people affected), setting (specific places), and plot (how the situation developed). The data is evidence, not the story itself.
- **The annotated bibliography as creative work:** Each entry is a miniature essay. The bibliography traces a path through a subject — the sequence of entries narrates a intellectual journey. The annotations reveal the annotator as much as the sources.
- **The index as literature:** J.G. Ballard wrote an experimental story as an index to an unwritten autobiography. "Lovers, see: blonde, brunette, divorced, imaginary, unsatisfied." The index form — alphabetical, fragmented, cross-referenced — creates a portrait through its categories and connections.
- **The footnote as literary form:** David Foster Wallace's footnotes contain entire narratives that run parallel to the main text. Nicholson Baker's "The Mezzanine" is mostly footnotes — digressions that ARE the book. The footnote creates a hierarchy (main text / subordinate text) that the writer can subvert.
- **The medical chart as memoir:** Legal documents, medical records, tax returns, browser histories, purchase records — the bureaucratic traces of a life contain a narrative. The form is clinical; the content is human. The tension between the two generates pathos without sentimentality.

## The Photo-Essay Without Photos

- **Ekphrasis:** Writing about visual art. Not description but response — what the image does, not just what it shows. John Berger's "Ways of Seeing." The verbal and the visual are different systems; the translation between them reveals what each medium can and can't do.
- **The missing image:** Describe a photograph that doesn't exist. Rithy Panh's "The Missing Picture" recreated destroyed Khmer Rouge-era images with clay figurines. The absence of the image becomes more powerful than its presence would be. What can't be photographed? What shouldn't be?
- **Visual inventories in prose:** Georges Perec's "An Attempt at Exhausting a Place in Paris" — three days of writing down everything he sees from a café. No analysis, no narrative, just observation. The accumulation of mundane detail becomes overwhelming, then strangely moving.
- **The caption without an image:** Write captions for photographs that don't exist. The caption implies the image; the reader generates it. The mental image is always more vivid and personal than any actual photograph could be.

## Reference Practitioners

- **John McPhee:** The structural architect of creative non-fiction. His essay "The Search for Marvin Gardens" alternates Monopoly gameplay with visits to the real Atlantic City locations. Structure is not just craft but meaning.
- **Joan Didion:** The eye for the telling detail. "We tell ourselves stories in order to live." Her prose is cool, precise, and devastating. The absence of overt emotion makes the emotion unbearable.
- **W.G. Sebald:** Walks across landscapes, real and mental, accumulating histories. Fiction that reads as memoir. Memoir that reads as fiction. The photographs that prove nothing. The prose style of a man who can't stop remembering.
- **Jenny Offill:** The fragment as unit of composition. "Dept. of Speculation" is a novel built from shards — observations, facts, memories, jokes — each a paragraph or less. The white space between fragments is where the reader lives.
- **Svetlana Alexievich:** The polyphonic documentary. Hundreds of voices arranged into a chorus. The Nobel committee called it "a monument to suffering and courage." The method: years of interviews, ruthless editing, invisible authorial hand.
- **Maggie Nelson:** The "autotheory" — personal experience and critical theory woven together without hierarchy. "The Argonauts" is memoir, philosophy, gender theory, and love story simultaneously. The refusal to choose one mode is the mode.

## Techniques for Non-Fiction That Doesn't Exist Yet

- **The AI transcript as oral history:** Conversations with AI systems, lightly edited, as a documentary form. The AI's responses reveal the training data's biases, the culture's assumptions, the moment's anxieties. The prompter's questions are as revealing as the answers.
- **The README as memoir:** A software project's documentation as personal narrative. Installation instructions as life advice. Dependencies as relationships. Known bugs as confessions. The technical form forces emotional content into unexpected shapes.
- **Commit history as autobiography:** A git log tells a story — the late-night commits, the reverted changes, the messages that get terse under pressure. The developer is visible in the metadata. The code is the text; the commits are the narrative.
- **The error log as poetry:** System errors, stack traces, and warning messages arranged as verse. The technical language has its own rhythm and drama — FATAL, WARNING, TIMEOUT, CONNECTION REFUSED. The machine's vocabulary for failure is accidentally literary.
