# Poetic Forms & Techniques Reference

## Fixed Forms

- **Sonnet (Shakespearean):** 14 lines, iambic pentameter, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. Three quatrains develop an argument; the closing couplet turns or resolves it. The volta (turn) is the poem's hinge — without it, you just have 14 lines.
- **Sonnet (Petrarchan):** 14 lines, ABBAABBA + CDECDE (or CDCDCD). The octave presents a problem or situation; the sestet responds. The volta falls between octave and sestet. More suited to meditation than argument.
- **Villanelle:** 19 lines, five tercets + one quatrain. Two refrains alternate as the final line of each tercet, then appear together at the end. The repetition creates obsessive circling — ideal for grief, fixation, things you can't let go of. ("Do not go gentle into that good night.")
- **Ghazal:** Couplets (5-15), each self-contained. The second line of every couplet ends with the same word or phrase (radif), preceded by a rhyme (qafia). The final couplet includes the poet's name. Themes: love, loss, longing. The couplet independence creates a constellation effect.
- **Pantoum:** Quatrains where lines 2 and 4 become lines 1 and 3 of the next stanza. Creates a woven, dreamlike quality. The recycled lines shift meaning in new contexts.
- **Haiku:** 5-7-5 syllables in English (though purists argue this). A concrete image from nature + a cut (kireji) that creates juxtaposition between two images or ideas. The cut is where the meaning lives. NOT a statement about nature — a moment of perception.
- **Senryu:** Same form as haiku but about human nature rather than nature. Wry, ironic, observational. Often darkly funny.
- **Tanka:** 5-7-5-7-7. The first three lines (kami-no-ku) set a scene; the last two (shimo-no-ku) respond with emotion or reflection. The pivot between them is where the poem happens.

## Free Verse Craft

- **Enjambment:** Breaking a line in the middle of a syntactic unit. "I wanted to / tell you" — the line break creates a micro-pause, a moment of suspended meaning. Where you break the line IS a choice with semantic weight.
- **Caesura:** A pause within a line, often marked by punctuation. "I am. I was. I will be." Creates rhythm through interruption. Effective for conveying fragmentation or emphasis.
- **White space as meaning:** The space around and between text is part of the poem. A gap between stanzas is a breath, a time-skip, a shift. Indentation can represent hierarchy, hesitation, or subordination.
- **Prose poetry:** Paragraph form, no line breaks as compositional tool. Uses every poetic device except lineation — density, image, rhythm, sound. The tension between prose form and poetic content IS the form.

## Sound Devices

- **Assonance:** Repeated vowel sounds — "the rain in Spain." Creates internal music, a sense of cohesion beneath the surface. Long vowels slow the line; short vowels speed it.
- **Consonance:** Repeated consonant sounds — "blank and think." Harder-edged than assonance. Plosives (b, d, k, t) create percussive rhythm. Sibilants (s, sh) create hissing or whispering.
- **Internal rhyme:** Rhyme within a line rather than at the end — "I bring the sting of spring." More subtle than end-rhyme, creates a sense of inevitability without sing-song.
- **Alliteration used well:** Binds words into phrases, creates emphasis. "Burned and buried" is powerful. But alliteration becomes parody quickly — "Peter Piper picked" is a tongue-twister, not poetry. Use to link 2-3 key words, not every word in a line.

## Image and Metaphor

- **Concrete image:** The foundation of poetry. Not "sadness" but "a dog waiting at a closed door." The image should be precise enough to see, hear, or feel. Abstraction is the enemy.
- **Extended metaphor:** A metaphor sustained across multiple lines or the entire poem. The tenor (subject) and vehicle (comparison) intertwine until the poem exists in both simultaneously.
- **Conceit:** An extended metaphor that yokes together dramatically unlike things — Donne's comparison of lovers to a compass. The surprise of the connection IS the insight.
- **Objective correlative (T.S. Eliot):** An external object, scene, or chain of events that evokes a specific emotion without naming it. Don't say "grief" — describe the untouched coffee going cold on the nightstand.

## Experimental Forms

- **Erasure poetry:** Take an existing text (newspaper, legal document, novel page) and black out most of it, leaving a poem in the remaining words. The source text constrains and enriches. The relationship between what's hidden and what's revealed creates meaning.
- **Found poetry:** Rearrange language from non-poetic sources — instruction manuals, spam emails, error messages, legal disclaimers — into poetic form. The reframing reveals latent beauty or horror in everyday language.
- **Oulipo constraints:** N+7 (replace every noun with the 7th noun after it in a dictionary). Lipogram (write without using a specific letter — Perec wrote an entire novel without the letter 'e'). The constraint forces the mind off familiar paths.
- **Concrete/visual poetry:** The typographic arrangement on the page IS part of the meaning. Apollinaire's calligrams shape text into images. Code poetry uses programming syntax as poetic form.

## Voice and Register

- **Diction as persona:** Word choice creates character. Anglo-Saxon monosyllables ("blood," "stone," "dark") feel primal. Latinate polysyllables ("ameliorate," "perspicacious") feel intellectual or distancing. Mixing registers within a poem creates tension or comedy.
- **Code-switching:** Moving between registers (formal/informal, technical/colloquial, languages) within a poem. Reflects how people actually think and speak. The shift itself carries meaning — who are you when you switch?
- **Formal vs. vernacular:** A sonnet in street slang. A text message in iambic pentameter. The friction between form and register generates energy. Neither is superior — the choice should serve the poem's purpose.
