# Speculative Design Reference

## Design Fiction

- **The diegetic prototype:** An object that exists within a fictional world — a prop that implies a complete technological, social, and economic context. The communicator in Star Trek isn't real, but it implies a world where communication is instant, portable, and universal. Design fiction creates the prop, then lets the viewer infer the world.
- **Design probes:** Deliberately provocative artifacts sent into the world to elicit responses. Not prototypes (which solve problems) but probes (which ask questions). A speculative product catalog for genetic modification services. A user manual for a device that doesn't exist. The reactions to the probe are the research data.
- **Artifacts from alternate presents:** Not futures — presents. What if the internet had been designed as a public utility? What would the browser look like? What if writing had evolved as 3D spatial notation instead of linear text? The artifact embodies the alternative without explaining it.
- **The future mundane:** Speculative design that focuses on the boring parts of the future. Not flying cars but the DMV paperwork for flying car registration. Not AI consciousness but the EULA you sign before activating your AI assistant. The mundane details make futures feel real.
- **Dunne & Raby's spectrum:** Probable futures (likely to happen) → Plausible futures (could happen) → Possible futures (might happen given some stretch) → Preferable futures (should happen). Speculative design operates in the plausible-to-possible range, making preferable futures tangible.

## Critical Design

- **Design as discourse:** Objects that argue. A chair designed to be deliberately uncomfortable in specific ways — forcing awareness of the body, the act of sitting, the politics of furniture. The object's dysfunction is its function.
- **The anti-solutionist stance:** Most design asks "how might we solve X?" Critical design asks "should we?" and "who benefits?" A perfectly designed surveillance camera is still a surveillance camera. Critical design makes the assumptions visible.
- **Discomfort as method:** Critical design should make you uneasy, not angry. It should provoke thought, not outrage. The difference between provocation and shock: provocation opens questions; shock closes them.
- **The Uncanny Valley of objects:** Familiar objects with something subtly wrong. A phone that's slightly too warm. A clock that occasionally skips a second. The familiarity makes the wrongness more disturbing than something entirely alien would be.
- **Black Mirror as design brief:** Each episode is essentially a design fiction — a near-future technology, a plausible social adoption pattern, and a consequence. The technology is never the villain; the human response is. This is the core insight of critical design: technology reveals us.

## Post-Human and Non-Human Design

- **Design for non-human users:** What does a well-designed bee highway look like? A comfortable space for a neural network? An interface for an octopus (distributed cognition, chromatophore communication, no bones)? Designing for non-human users forces you to examine every anthropocentric assumption in your design vocabulary.
- **Biomimicry as philosophy, not technique:** Not "copy the lotus leaf to make a waterproof surface" but "what would design look like if it followed biological principles?" Growth instead of manufacturing. Adaptation instead of versioning. Decomposition instead of disposal. Symbiosis instead of competition.
- **Post-human interfaces:** Design for augmented bodies, distributed consciousness, non-linear time perception. If memory were perfect, would you need bookmarks? If your sensorium extended into infrared, how would color palettes change? If you could fork into parallel instances, what would a calendar look like?
- **Xenodesign:** Design informed by the radically other. Slime mold optimizes transport networks. Mycelium networks distribute resources. Coral reefs self-organize. These aren't metaphors — they're design principles from organisms that solve complex problems without brains, intentions, or prototypes.

## Afrofuturism, Indigenous Futurism, and Other Futurisms

- **Afrofuturism:** The intersection of Black culture, technology, and science fiction. Sun Ra, Octavia Butler, Black Panther's Wakanda. Not just "Black people in space" but a reframing of who gets to imagine the future and from what cultural foundation. Technology rooted in African aesthetic and philosophical traditions rather than European industrial ones.
- **Indigenous futurism:** Futures that don't require abandoning Indigenous epistemologies. Circular time rather than linear progress. Technology as relationship rather than tool. Land as participant rather than resource. The future is also the past — ancestors and descendants coexist in the design space.
- **Solarpunk:** An optimistic counter to cyberpunk. Technology in service of ecology, community, and beauty. Solar panels as stained glass. Vertical gardens as architecture. Repair culture instead of replacement culture. The aesthetic is "Art Nouveau meets appropriate technology."
- **Critical fabulation (Saidiya Hartman):** Imagining the lives that the archive erased. Filling gaps in historical records with speculative narrative that honors what was lost. Applied to design: creating artifacts for histories that were suppressed, cultures that were disrupted, futures that were stolen.

## Speculative Interfaces

- **The interface as ideology:** Every interface embodies assumptions. A hierarchical file system assumes knowledge is tree-structured. A search bar assumes you can articulate what you're looking for. A timeline assumes linear chronology. Speculative interfaces make these assumptions visible by violating them.
- **Spatial computing as world-building:** AR/VR aren't just display technologies — they're world-building tools. A speculative AR layer that shows the historical use of every building you pass. A VR environment that simulates a different species' perceptual world (mantis shrimp vision, echolocation).
- **The Museum of the Future (Dubai):** A functioning speculative design exhibition. Not artifacts behind glass but immersive environments you walk through — a future rainforest, a future space station, a future biotech lab. The architecture itself is the argument.
- **Paper prototypes of impossible interfaces:** Sketch an interface for communicating with a future AI that has no concept of discrete conversation turns. Sketch a music player for someone who experiences all of time simultaneously. The sketch is the speculation.

## Methods and Exercises

- **The thing from the future:** A combinatorial card game (from Situation Lab). Draw cards for: future (type/distance), terrain (topic), object (form), mood (emotional quality). "An optimistic artifact from a near-future collapse about education." The random combination forces unexpected speculation.
- **Backcasting:** Start with a desired future state. Work backward: what had to happen the year before? And before that? Down to next Monday. The chain of causation becomes a design brief for intervention points.
- **World-building as research method:** Don't design a product; design the world in which the product makes sense. Who made it? Who regulates it? Who can't afford it? What's the black market version? What do critics say about it? The product is a lens into a complete social system.
- **The prop box:** Create a collection of physical (or digital) artifacts from your speculative world. A receipt from a future store. A warning label from a speculative product. A screenshot of a UI that doesn't exist yet. Accumulate props until the world becomes real enough to walk around in.

### What if...

- ...you designed the user manual for a device that translates between human languages and whale song — what assumptions would the FAQ section reveal?
- ...you created the Terms of Service for a time-travel tourism company — what liabilities would they disclaim?
- ...you wrote the angry 1-star product review for a consumer brain-computer interface — what would go wrong in mundane, relatable ways?
- ...you designed the packaging for a product from a future where intellectual property doesn't exist — how would branding work?
- ...you created an artifact from a future where AI art is the norm and human-made art is the luxury good — what does the authentication certificate look like?
