# Writing Techniques Reference

## Narrative Point of View

- **First person unreliable:** The narrator's account contradicts evidence the reader can see. Creates tension between what's said and what's true. Use when the gap between perception and reality IS the story.
- **Second person:** "You walk into the room." Collapses distance between reader and character. Powerful for immersion, complicity, or accusation. Works best in short form — sustained second person fatigues.
- **Close third (free indirect discourse):** Third person that bleeds into the character's voice and thoughts without attribution. "She opened the fridge. God, was there nothing to eat in this house?" The gold standard for literary fiction.
- **Omniscient:** Narrator knows everything, can enter any mind, can editorialize. Fell out of fashion but remains powerful for irony and scope. The narrator becomes a character.
- **Objective/camera eye:** Reports only observable action and dialogue. No interiority. Forces the reader to infer emotion from behavior. Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants" is the canonical example.

## Structural Techniques

- **In medias res:** Begin in the middle of action. Orient the reader through context, not exposition. The opening scene should raise a question the reader needs answered.
- **Frame narrative:** A story contains a story. The outer frame recontextualizes the inner story. Useful when the act of telling is as important as what's told.
- **Epistolary/found document:** Letters, emails, reports, chat logs, error messages. The format constrains voice naturally and implies a world beyond the text.
- **Braided timelines:** Two or more timelines intercut. Each illuminates the other. The juxtaposition IS the meaning — if rearranging the sections changes nothing, the braiding isn't working.
- **Reverse chronology:** Start at the end, work backward. Each scene recontextualizes what the reader already knows. Effect becomes cause. "Memento" structure.
- **Collage/fragment:** Discontinuous sections that accumulate into meaning. White space between sections does narrative work. Trust the reader to assemble.

## Prose-Level Craft

- **Concrete detail:** "A red 1987 Toyota Corolla with a cracked windshield" beats "an old car." Specific details create belief. Pick details that do double duty — reveal character, set mood, advance plot.
- **Showing vs. telling:** "Her hands shook" vs. "She was nervous." Show when the moment matters; tell when you need to cover ground quickly. The real skill is knowing when to do which.
- **Sentence rhythm:** Vary length. Short sentences create urgency. Long sentences with embedded clauses and careful subordination can slow the reader down, building a kind of sustained attention that mirrors contemplation. Then short again. Like breathing.
- **Dialogue that reveals:** Every line of dialogue should do at least two of: advance plot, reveal character, create tension. If a line only conveys information, cut it or make a character resist giving the information.
- **Subtext:** What characters don't say matters more than what they do. Two people discussing the weather while their marriage falls apart.

## Flash Fiction Constraints

- **Sudden fiction (under 1500 words):** A complete emotional arc compressed. Often a single scene, single moment of change.
- **Drabble (exactly 100 words):** The constraint forces radical economy. Every word must earn its place.
- **Six-word stories:** "For sale: baby shoes, never worn." The reader's imagination fills the void. The story lives in implication.
- **Constrained writing as creative fuel:** Lipograms (omit a letter), monovocalics (one vowel only), N+7 (replace nouns with the 7th noun after them in a dictionary). Constraints bypass the editor brain and force invention.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Said-bookisms:** "he exclaimed," "she retorted," "they ejaculated." Just use "said" — it's invisible. Or better, use action beats: "She set down the glass. 'I don't think so.'"
- **Purple prose:** Overwrought description that calls attention to itself rather than its subject. If the prose is more interesting than what it describes, something's wrong.
- **Info dumps:** Characters explaining things they both already know for the reader's benefit. Deliver exposition through conflict — people reveal information when they're arguing, confessing, or trying to persuade.
- **Deus ex machina:** A solution that wasn't set up. The gun on the mantelpiece in act one must fire by act three — but the reverse is also true: the gun that fires in act three must have been visible in act one.
